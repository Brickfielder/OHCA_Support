Survivors and Family members

Surviving a cardiac arrest is life-changing, and the journey doesn't end once a person leaves the hospital. Many survivors and their families face significant ongoing challenges, including persistent fatigue, memory difficulties, emotional distress, anxiety, or depression.

However, finding help can be difficult due to a lack of clear information about available services. The aim of this page is to ensure survivors and their loved ones can find and access the support they need, reducing isolation and improving recovery and quality of life.

Resuscitation Council UK (RCUK) has created Survivorship Quality Standards to make sure everyone who survives a cardiac arrest‚Äîand those who support them‚Äîgets the right help at the right time, wherever they live.

They recommend:

  • Checks before leaving hospital for physical, memory/thinking, and emotional needs.
  • Clear plans and referrals to rehabilitation, psychological support, and other services.
  • Follow-up around 3 months after discharge for survivors and their supporters.
  • Support for family and friends who may also be affected.
  • Easy routes back into care if new problems appear later.

This directory helps turn those standards into reality by showing where follow-up and aftercare are available across the UK ‚Äî and where it is not yet available, it helps to highlight gaps in the service.

Bystanders/Rescuers

We estimate that 1‚Äì5 bystanders are involved in cardiac arrest situations on average, though many of these events go unwitnessed. Encountering such an event can be difficult and distressing, whether you are a family member or a stranger. You might feel numb, distressed, exhausted or perhaps completely fine after your experience. You may also find it difficult to think clearly.

Whatever emotions you're experiencing, there are now a handful of services that can provide a debrief, initial support, and signpost tailored to your specific needs.
